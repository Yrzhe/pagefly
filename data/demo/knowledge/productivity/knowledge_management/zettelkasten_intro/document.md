# Zettelkasten: The Slip-Box Method

The Zettelkasten (German for "slip-box") is a note-taking system popularized by
the sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who used it to produce over 70 books and 400
academic articles. The core idea is simple but powerful: instead of organizing
notes by topic or source, you organize them by **idea**, and you link ideas
together explicitly.

## The three types of notes

1. **Fleeting notes** — quick captures from daily life. These are disposable.
2. **Literature notes** — summaries of things you've read, in your own words,
   with the source attached.
3. **Permanent notes** — atomic ideas, one per note, written as if for a future
   reader. These are linked to related permanent notes.

The magic is in the links. Luhmann's slip-box wasn't a tree, it was a graph. A
note could be linked to many other notes, and new ideas would emerge from the
connections.

## Atomic notes

The most important rule: **one idea per note**. If a note contains two ideas,
split it. This forces you to name each idea clearly, and it makes linking
precise. A link should say "this specific idea connects to that specific idea,"
not "these two topics are vaguely related."

## Why this is hard without tools

Maintaining a Zettelkasten by hand requires discipline. Every note needs a
unique ID, every link needs to be manually maintained, and as the collection
grows it becomes hard to remember what's already in there. Digital tools
(Obsidian, Roam, Logseq) make the mechanics easier but still require the human
to do all the linking and all the synthesis.

# Spaced Repetition and Long-Term Memory

Spaced repetition is a learning technique where information is reviewed at
increasing intervals over time. The technique exploits a well-documented
phenomenon in cognitive psychology: the **spacing effect**, first described by
Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885.

## The forgetting curve

Ebbinghaus's experiments on himself showed that memory decays exponentially
after learning. Without review, recall drops to about 40% within a day and
continues to decrease. But each review flattens the curve — after several
well-timed reviews, the information stabilizes in long-term memory.

## Why it works

Every time you successfully recall a piece of information **just before you
would have forgotten it**, the recall itself strengthens the memory trace.
Recall is harder than recognition, and the effort of reconstruction is what
consolidates memory. This is sometimes called **desirable difficulty**.

The algorithm that modern tools (Anki, SuperMemo) use schedules the next review
based on how easy or hard the recall was. Easy cards get pushed further out;
hard cards come back sooner.

## Relevance to knowledge management

A personal knowledge base only delivers value if you can recall what's in it.
Capture without recall is just digital hoarding. This is the case for building
active review into any PKM system — whether through daily review notes, weekly
summaries, or spaced-repetition flashcards generated from the wiki itself.
